Exercise 1

Analyse the logical forms of the following statements:

a

We'll have either a reading assignment or homework problems, but we won't have both homework problems and a test.

Solution

If we let \(R\) stand for the statement "we'll have a reading assignment" as and let \(H\) stand for the statement "we'll have homework problems" then we can write the statement as \[(R \lor H) \land \neg (R \land H)\]

b

You won't go skiing, or you will and there won't be any snow.

Solution

If we let \(K\) stand for the statement "you will go skiing" and let \(S\) stand for the statement "there will be snow" then we can write the statement as \[\neg K \lor (K \land \neg S)\]

c

\[\sqrt{7} \nleq 2\]

Solution

If we let \(A\) be \(\sqrt{7} \lt 2\) and let B be \(\sqrt{7} = 2\) then we can write the statement as \[\neg(A \land B)\]

Exercise 2

Analyze the logical forms of the following statements:

a

Either John and Bill are both telling the truth, or neither of them is.

Solution

If we let \(J\) stand for the statement "John tells the truth" and let \(B\) stand for the statement "Bill tells the truth" then we can write the statement as \[(J \land B) \lor (\neg J \land \neg B)\]

b

I'll have either fish or chicken, but I won't have both fish and mashed potatoes.

Solution

If we let \(F\) stand for the statement "I'll have fish" and let \(C\) stand for the statement "I'll have chicken" and let \(M\) stand for the statement "I'll have mashed potatoes" then we can write the statement as \[(F \lor C) \land \neg (F \land M)\]

c

3 is a common divisor of 6, 9 and 15

Solution

If we let \(A\) be "\(3\) divides \(6\)" and let \(B\) be "\(3\) divides \(9\) and let \(C\) be "\(3\) divides \(15\)" then we can write the statement as \[A \land B \land C\]

Exercise 3

Analyze the logical forms of the following statements:

a

Alice and Bob are not both in the room.

Solution

If we let \(A\) stand for "Alice is in the room" and let \(B\) stand for "Bob is in the room" then we can write the statement as \[\neg (A \land B)\]

b

Alice and Bob are both not in the room.

Solution

If we let \(A\) stand for "Alice is in the room" and let \(B\) stand for "Bob is in the room" then we can write the statement as \[(\neg A \land \neg B)\]

c

Either Alice or Bob is not in the room

Solution

If we let \(A\) stand for "Alice is in the room" and let \(B\) stand for "Bob is in the room" then we can write the statement as \[\neg A \lor \neg B\]

d

Neither Alice or Bob is in the room

Solution

If we let \(A\) stand for "Alice is in the room" and let \(B\) stand for "Bob is in the room" then we can write the statement as \[(\neg A \land \neg B)\]

Exercise 4

Analyze the logical forms of the following statements:

a

Either both Ralph and Ed are tall, or both of them are handsome.

Solution

If we let \(R\) stand for "Ralph is tall", \(E\) for "Ed is tall", \(S\) for "Ralph is handsome" and \(F\) for "Ed is handsome" then we can write the statement as \[(R \land E) \lor (S \land F)\]

b

Both Ralph and Ed are either tall or handsome.

Solution

If we let \(R\) stand for "Ralph is tall", \(E\) for "Ed is tall", \(S\) for "Ralph is handsome" and \(F\) for "Ed is handsome" then we can write the statement as \[(R \lor S) \land (E \lor F) \land \neg (R \land S) \land \neg (E \land F)\]

c

Both Ralph and Ed are neither tall nor handsome

Solution

If we let \(R\) stand for "Ralph is tall", \(E\) for "Ed is tall", \(S\) for "Ralph is handsome" and \(F\) for "Ed is handsome" then we can write the statement as \[\neg R \land \neg E \land \neg S \land \neg F\]

d

Neither Ralph nor Ed is both tall and handsome

Solution

If we let \(R\) stand for "Ralph is tall", \(E\) for "Ed is tall", \(S\) for "Ralph is handsome" and \(F\) for "Ed is handsome" then we can write the statement as \[\neg (R \land S) \land \neg (E \land F)\]

Exercise 5

Which of the following expressions are well-formed formulas?

a

\[\neg (\neg P \lor \neg \neg R)\]

Solution

Well-formed

b

\[\neg (P, Q, \land R)\]

Solution

Not well-formed. Comma's are not part of the language of sentential logic.

c

\[P \land \neg P\]

Solution

Well-formed

d

\[(P \land Q)(P \lor R)\]

Solution

Not well-formed. There should be a logical connective in between \()(\).

Exercise 6

Let \(P\) stand for the statement "I will buy the pants" and \(S\) for the statement "I will buy the shirt". What English sentences are represented by the following formulas?

a

\[\neg (P \land \neg S)\]

Solution

I won't buy the pants and not buy the shirt.

b

\[\neg P \land \neg S\]

Solution

I won't buy the pants and I won't buy the shirt.

c

\[\neg P \lor \neg S\]

Solution

I won't buy the pants or I won't buy the shirt.

Exercise 7

Let \(S\) stand for the statement "Steve is happy" and \(G\) for "George is happy." What English sentences are represented by the following formulas?

a

\[(S \lor G) \land (\neg S \lor \neg G)\]

Solution

From Steve and George, one is happy and one isn't.

b

\[[S \lor (G \land \neg S)] \lor \neg G\]

Solution

This statement is always true.

c

\[S \lor [G \land (\neg S \lor \neg G)\]

Solution

Steve is happy or George is happy or both are.

Exercise 8

Let \(T\) stand for the statement "Taxes will go up" and \(D\) for "The deficit will go up." What English sentences are represented by the following formulas?

a

\[T \lor D\]

Solution

Taxes will go up or the deficit will go up (or both).

b

\[\neg (T \land D) \lor \neg (\neg T \land \neg D)\]

Solution

\[\begin{aligned} &\equiv \neg (T \land D) \lor (T \lor D)\\ &\equiv \neg T \lor \neg D \lor T \lor D\\ &\equiv True\\ \end{aligned}\] This statement is always true.

c

\[(T \land \neg D) \lor (D \land \neg T)\]

Solution

Either taxes will go up or the defict will go up (and not both).

Exercise 9

Identify the premises and conclusions of the following deductive arguments and analyze their logical forms. Do you think the reasoning is valid? (Although you will have only your intuition to guide you in answering this last question, in the next section we will develop some techniques for determining the validity of arguments.)

a

Jane and Pete won't both win the same math prize. Pete will win either the math prize or the chemistry prize. Jane will win the math prize. Therefore, Pete will win the chemistry prize.

Solution

If we let \(M(J)\) stand for the statement "Jane will win the math prize", \(C(J)\) stand for the statement "Jane will win the chemistry prize", \(M(P)\) stand for the statement "Pete will win the math prize" and \(C(P)\) stand for the statement "Pete will win the chemistry prize". Then we can write the problem statement as \[\neg(M(J) \land M(P)) \land (M(P) \lor C(P)) \land M(J) \implies C(P)\] The reasoning is valid.

b

The main course will be either beef or fish. The vegetable will be either peas or corn. We will not have both fish as a main course and corn as a vegetable. Therefore, we will not have both beef as a main course and peas as a vegetable.

Solution

If we let \(B\) stand for the statement "the main course will be beef", \(F\) for the satement "the main course will be fish", \(P\) for the statement "the vegetable will be peas" and \(C\) for the statement "the vegetable will be corn". Then we can write the problem statement as \[(B \oplus F) \land (P \oplus C) \land \neg (F \land C) \implies \neg (B \land P)\] We can still have fish and peas, beef and peas and beef and corn. The reasoning is wrong.

c

Either John or Bill is telling the truth. Either Sam or Bill is lying. Therefore, either John is telling the truth or Sam is lying.

Solution

If we let \(J\) stand for the statement "John is telling the truth" , \(B\) stand for the statement "Bill is telling the truth" and \(S\) stand for the statement "Sam is telling the truth". Then we can rewrite the problem statement as \[(J \oplus B) \land (\neg S \oplus \neg B) \implies (J \oplus \neg S)\]. We can rewrite the premise as \[(J \land \neg S) \oplus (J \land \neg B) \oplus (B \land \neg S) \] It is possible that \(J \land \neg S\) therefore the conclusion is wrong.

d

Either sales will go up and the boss will be happy, or expenses will go up and the boss won't be happy. Therefore, sales and expenses will not both go up.

Solution

If we let \(S\) stand for the statement "sales will go up", \(E\) for the statement "expenses will go up" and \(B\) for the statement "the boss is happy" then we can rewrite the problem statement as \[(S \land B) \oplus (E \land \neg B) \implies \neg (S \land E)\] This reasoning is correct.
